## What You Need to Start Canning (The Honest List)

Before I walk you through the jam, let me give you the real list — no fluff, no overwhelm.

**Equipment:**
- A large stockpot (at least 8 quarts) with a lid
- A jar rack or a folded kitchen towel to set jars on in the pot
- Mason jars with new lids and bands (pint jars for jam)
- A ladle and a wide-mouth funnel
- Jar lifter tongs (under $10 at any grocery store)
- A clean cloth for wiping jar rims

**That's it.** You do not need a pressure canner for jam. You do not need a $200 canning kit. You need a big pot, some jars, and a willing afternoon.

## How to Make Strawberry Jam: The Method That Never Fails

This is the method I taught my granddaughters, and the same one Marjorie used on her first try. It's classic, tested, and it works.

### Ingredients (Makes about 8 pint jars)

- 8 cups crushed strawberries (about 4 pounds whole)
- 6 cups granulated sugar
- 1/4 cup fresh lemon juice
- 1 box (1.75 oz) powdered pectin

### Step 1: Sterilize Your Jars

Fill your stockpot with water and bring it to a boil. Submerge your clean jars and let them sit in the hot water while you prepare the jam. Keep them warm until you're ready to fill — cold jars crack.

### Step 2: Crush Your Strawberries

Hull and crush your strawberries one layer at a time using a potato masher. You want texture, not puree. If you crush them all the way to liquid, your jam won't have that lovely bit of fruit in every spoonful.

### Step 3: Cook the Jam

Combine crushed strawberries, lemon juice, and pectin in a large saucepan. Bring to a full rolling boil over high heat, stirring constantly. Add sugar all at once. Return to a full rolling boil and boil hard for exactly 1 minute, stirring the whole time. Remove from heat. Skim off any foam with a spoon.

### Step 4: Fill Your Jars

Use your jar lifter to remove the warm jars from the stockpot. Set them on a folded towel. Using your ladle and funnel, fill each jar leaving 1/4 inch headspace at the top. Wipe the rims clean with a damp cloth. This step matters — any jam on the rim can prevent a seal.

Place the lids and bands on. Finger-tight only. Not cranked down.

### Step 5: Process in the Water Bath

Lower filled jars into the boiling water bath. Water should cover the jars by at least 1 inch. Bring back to a boil. Process for 10 minutes (adjust for altitude if needed — add 1 minute per 1,000 feet above sea level).

### Step 6: Listen for the Ping

Remove jars and set them on a towel to cool undisturbed for 12–24 hours. As they cool, you will hear a satisfying *ping* — that's the lid sealing. Each ping is a small victory.

After 24 hours, press down on each lid. If it doesn't flex, it's sealed. Any that flex go in the fridge and get eaten first.

## Frequently Asked Questions About Canning for Beginners

**Q: Is home canning safe for beginners?**
A: Yes, when you follow tested recipes and proper technique. Water-bath canning — used for high-acid foods like jams, jellies, tomatoes, and pickles — has been practiced safely for over 200 years. The key is using approved recipes, proper headspace, and processing times. RootedReadyLife's Preserve course teaches all of this step by step.

**Q: Do I need special equipment to start canning?**
A: No. You can start with a large stockpot, mason jars with new lids, and a ladle. A jar lifter and wide-mouth funnel (under $15 total) make the process easier and safer. You do not need a pressure canner to make jams, jellies, or pickles.

**Q: How long does homemade strawberry jam last?**
A: Properly sealed jars stored in a cool, dark pantry will last 12–18 months. Once opened, refrigerate and use within 3 weeks. If a jar doesn't seal (the lid flexes when pressed), put it in the fridge and use it within 2 weeks.

**Q: Can I can with my kids or grandkids?**
A: Absolutely. Children can help wash strawberries, mash fruit, label jars, and listen for the ping. Keep them away from the boiling water bath for safety, but there's plenty of meaningful work for small hands. Many of our students say canning together became a family tradition.

**Q: What's the easiest thing to can as a complete beginner?**
A: Strawberry jam is our top recommendation — high natural acid, short processing time, and very forgiving. Other great beginner options: blueberry jam, bread and butter pickles, and applesauce. All three are covered in the RootedReadyLife Preserve course.
