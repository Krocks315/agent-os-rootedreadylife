## EMAIL 2: The 4-Crop Garden
**Subject:** You don't need a farm. You need four crops.
**Send:** Day 2

Hey again —

Yesterday I told you about my empty pantry and my abuela's garden.

Today I want to talk about the moment everything clicked for me on the growing side — because gardening was the thing I was most scared of. I had NEVER grown anything on purpose. Not even a houseplant that survived longer than a month.

Here's what I finally figured out:

You don't need to grow everything. You need to grow four things.

I call it the 4-Crop Garden, and it works whether you have a backyard, a patio, a balcony, or a back porch with two hours of afternoon sun.

The four crops:

Tomatoes — the anchor. One plant gives you more than you expect. Grow them in a container if you have to. They will find a way.

Herbs — specifically basil, mint, and one medicinal herb (I started with chamomile because it's basically impossible to kill). Fresh herbs change how you cook. Dried herbs change how you feel.

Cucumbers — fast growers, high yield, and you can pickle them which means you're now a food preserver. Congratulations.

Greens — lettuce, kale, spinach — something you can cut and eat fresh all summer. Instant return on your effort.

That's it. Not 40 varieties. Not a raised bed system. Four crops. One season. See what happens.

One of our students, Hannah, started with two tomato plants in containers on her apartment back porch. She sent me a photo of her first ripe tomato with the caption "I GREW A TOMATO." I printed it. It's on my wall.

That's how it starts. One tomato. One summer. And then you want to know what else you can do.

What do you have space for where you are right now?

## EMAIL 3: The 12 Herbs
**Subject:** Your medicine cabinet is missing something
**Send:** Day 4

Okay, today we're going into the herb garden, and I promise this is not as intimidating as it sounds.

When I first heard "medicinal herbs," I pictured some kind of woodland situation with a cauldron. I'm a Bronx girl. We don't do cauldrons. We do bodegas.

But here's the thing — the herbs I'm talking about? Your grandmother probably used them. Maybe not by name. But she had them somewhere, and she called them "what you put in the tea when somebody had a stomach ache."

There are 12 herbs I consider essential for any household. Not 200. Just 12. Most of them you can grow in a pot on your windowsill.

Here are three to start with:

Chamomile — the most forgiving plant I've ever encountered. Makes a tea that actually calms you down. Good for sleep, anxiety, upset stomachs. Nearly impossible to kill. I respect it deeply.

Peppermint — put it in a container (it will take over your garden if you let it in the ground, I say this with love). Digestive support, headache relief, and it makes everything smell incredible. It's doing SO much.

Lemon balm — calming, antiviral, tastes like sunshine. Great in tea, great fresh in water, great dried. Grows fast. Hard to mess up.

You don't need to become an herbalist. You just need to know what's in your tea.

Start with chamomile on a windowsill. That's the whole first step.

Talk soon,
Lyra

*P.S. Chamomile, lemon balm, and peppermint are all safe around kids and grandkids. Let them help water. They'll actually use the tea later.*
